# Home Phone, Phone Home
TAGLINE: Overhauling our access control system for Hack Night with a new, vintage touch.

OUTLINE:

Every Friday night, a seemingly nonstop stream of people want to enter Hack Night, our flagship weekly hacker festival where people make cool shit, to join in on the relentless building. However, our venue, the Bechtel Center, presents us with a problem: restrictive access control. Only a select few members of Purdue Hackers are allowed access to the building through the built-in systems.

We’ve had this problem ever since we moved into the space, and over time we’ve come up with two solutions. The first was the doorbell, a web page that, when rung, would also ring every other browser on that page. When paired with a device on loudspeakers where everyone was working and a QR code on the front doors to the venue, this worked reasonably well. The big drawback was that it was always a hassle to run downstairs to manually open the door.

[DOORBELL + QR CODE IF WE HAVE IT]

Later down the line, we started the Passports initiative [LINK], small faux leather booklets modeled after real passports that contain an NFC chip. One of the benefits of these passports is any device could read them on its own to determine if the holder is a valid member of Purdue Hackers. Out of this concept came the door opener. The door opener is a Raspberry Pi-powered, NFC-enabled device that is suction cupped onto the front doors. When a passport holder aligns their passport with the device, the door opener opens the door automatically with the help of a small linear actuator on the nearby ADA button.

[DOOR OPENER]

We’ve updated the door opener multiple times at this point, switching from a servo to a linear actuator, making the button pusher wireless using an ESP32, and overhauling the enclosure to accommodate a Pi 5 instead of a Pi 3B, a much bigger display, and a camera.

[THE NEW DOOR OPENER ENCLOSURE]

The door opener made our lives so much easier. Regular members could just scan their passport and come in without us having to send someone downstairs to open the door. However, there was one big issue with our new system: what about the members who don’t have passports? Passports are in high demand and short supply, so we couldn’t just give one to everyone who wanted one. Instead, we would have to think outside the box, to a completely different technology.

# Building a Prototype

[JACK START REVIEW HERE]

There were two main methods we could have used to control the phones, we could make a module that plugs in to the phone line to convert any phone, or we could tightly integrate the electronics in to the phone. We discussed this, and the idea of making the phones simple units you plug a USB cord in to was both appealing from the standpoint of making them simple to use, and funny from the standpoint of telling people that we have "USB rotary phones". Given this, the first thing she did was to disassemble the phones and figure out how to hook up to them internally.

Rotary phones come from an era of a significantly simpler telephone exchanges. The dials used a method called pulse dialing, where the phone would send the number of pluses corresponding to each number. The phone would send one pulse for the number one, two pulses for two, and so on up to ten pulses for the number zero. Combined with some basic electronics inside the phone that would change the voltages on the telephone lines according to what the handset was doing, the phone would physically move parts inside a telephone exchange to connect a call. When you picked up the phone, the voltage on the line would trigger a mechanism to connect you to a machine called a "step-by-step switch". This switch had a mechanism that would listen for the number of pulses and connect you to an open telephone line. Each switch had a tier of telephone lines that would correspond to the number you dialed, and each pulse told the switch to move to the next tier. This repeated process of moving to the correct tier, sweeping across the lines on that tier to find an open one, and repeating until a whole number was dialed would eventually get the switch to physically connect you to the person who you were calling, at which point the phones would switch internally and connect their microphones and speakers together to the other end until the call was ended.

These switches usually ran at around 10 pulses per second, which in terms of modern computers is monumentally slow. This is good though, because it meant we didn't need any specialized hardware to decode the pulses and we could use the GPIO of a computer or a microcontroller of some sort to decode them! Hazel disassembled the phones and found all the wiring nicely connected to the internal relay logic with screw terminals, making it easy to disassemble and probe. She eventually had both phones mapped out, with the wires corresponding to the dialer's idle, and pulse switches, (the idle switch would tell the phone if the dialer was actively being used, and the pulse switch would generate the pulses for each number) the switch for the handset, the solenoid for the bell, and the microphone and speaker of the handset all labeled. Next came choosing something to hook them up two. She had some old Raspberry Pi 3B+ single board computers from an old project that she wanted to use both because the overkill processing power and network capability of the SoC would make the firmware easy to develop, and the Linux kernels' battle tested networking and audio stack would avoid having to spend time optimizing something to fit on to a smaller microcontroller. Finally, she needed to choose the full software stack. Since she was using a Raspberry Pi and had recently fully picked up Rust as a programming language, she decided to go with the minimal install of Raspberry Pi OS running on the Pi's, and to write both the phone bell software and the server that managed them in Rust. This is when prototyping truly started, she began to assemble the hardware together with some basic testing software on the Pi to make the hardware completely work before linking the phones together.

First up was getting the dialing to work. She cut off the screw terminals and crimped JST connectors on to the wires for the idle and pulse lines. After connecting one of the lines on each switch to a common 3.3 volt source (since the Raspberry Pi's GPIO uses a 3.3 volt logic level and has a max voltage that's not too much higher) she connected the other end to two random GPIO pins that wouldn't get used and added pull down resistors to prevent the line from floating. She then wrote some code that would:

1. Wait for the idle line to be pulled high (indicating that dialing has started)
2. Count the number of times the pulse line is pulled low
3. Stop counting once the idle line has been pulled low (indicating that dialing has ended)
4. Print out the number of pulses it counted

This logic seemed all good, but it quickly had problems: during each dial the phone would rack up hundreds, if not thousands of pulses. This resulted in a mystery, clearly the contacts inside the dialer would bounce, *a lot*, but why would they bounce so much. In electronics, bouncing is a phenomenon where when two contacts come together, the springiness of the contacts will cause them to briefly bounce against each other many times. For humans, this is almost invisible, but for the high-speed GPIO circuitry inside a computer, it was able to see every bounce. This still didn't explain the scale of the bounce though. Normally, microcontrollers would see maybe 5 to 10 bounces, not hundreds. Turns out, the GPIO circuitry inside the BCM2837B0 (the SoC that drives the Raspberry Pi 3B+) uses something called an edge detector. Instead of detecting if a GPIO is high or low, it saves cost and transistor space to simply check if the GPIO *switched* from low to high or high to low and then set a register in the CPU telling it that something changed. In order to reduce the latency of the GPIO readings, broadcom made the detector very sensitive. This meant that even small analogue fluctuations well within the high and low voltage ranges of the GPIO would trigger an edge detection. What was being seen was not only the bouncing of the switches inside the dial, but also the capacitance of the line smoothing out the voltage fluctuations but also causing multiple edge events per dial pulse. Regardless of the source and cause, the fix was the same: debouncing. She decided to use software debouncing, both because it eliminated the probability that electrical changes would throw off the tuning of a physical debounce circuit, and because she decided that being able to modify the debounce strength later in software if needed would be significantly easier and more maintainable than requiring electrical changes. After adding the debouncing and testing again, she was able to see numbers being print to a terminal as she dialed them on the phone!

Next up, was controlling the bell solenoid. Back when these phones were on the telephone network, an on-hook phone would have it's ringer solenoid connected directly to the phone lines. When someone called a phone, the network would send a 15 to 25 Hz signal somewhere between 60 to 105 volts AC to the ringing phone. This would cause the solenoid to oscillate back and fourth between two internal bells causing the characteristic ringing sound. Of course, here we needed to somehow drive this solenoid with a significantly lower voltage, around 5 volts coming from a power supply. This presents a major problem: the resistance of a solenoid is generally tuned based on the input voltage to target a specific magnetic field strength and current draw. This is a problem, since the high resistance of the coil meant trying to apply 5 volts directly didn't even make it budge. The first solution she looked at was raising the system power supply voltage and using a regulator for the Raspberry Pi. The eventual goal was to use a USB Power Delivery power supply, so there were a few voltage options. The highest voltage she could get at the time with chargers and widely available ICs rated for the USB Power Delivery 3.0 specification was 20 volts. Sadly however, even this voltage level wasn't enough to make the bell ring, although it would finally move. She did briefly consider purchasing more advanced chargers and ICs fully capable of USB Power Delivery 3.1 for 48 volt charging, but options didn't really exist for anything reasonable at the time and would have backed the phone in to requiring expensive and specialized chargers. The next option was to operate at a normal voltage and somehow generate a higher voltage. Luckily, there are already a selection of circuits that can do this job, and from previous tests when considering 48 volts, she knew that it was enough to fully ring the bell, especially when driving the solenoid near the resonant frequency of the hammer. She decided on using an off-the-shelf boost converter circuit for testing. After finding one capable of going from 5 volts to around 48 volts, she hooked it up and found that it was able to perfectly drive the bell!

The next thing to do was to make the bell oscillate on command. She had been testing by manually touching wires together to make the bell move, but we now needed a way to have the Raspberry Pi make those connections. The first big thing she considered when deciding on a method was if the solenoid needed to be driven back and fourth or if the integrated spring would be enough to return it and still make a sound. When comparing the two manually, being able to move the bell both ways produced a significantly clearer sound on both strikes as opposed to there being a loud strike and then either a very quiet or even inaudible strike. The need to drive the solenoid in both directions made one type of driver an obvious choice: a full H-bridge driver. An H-bridge driver is a special arrangement of transistors that allows for a DC voltage on a wire to be reversed on command. By using two transistors on each side of your load, (in this case the solenoid) with one on each side connected to positive voltage and one on each side connected to ground, you can turn them on in opposite pairs to generate a voltage between them. With this in mind, she needed to decide on an implementation. The two options were either to implement the H-bridge from scratch with four transistors (in this case, MOSFETs) with the applicable driving amplification and protection circuitry, or using an all-in-one integrated H-bridge driver IC. Comparing the two solutions and considering the solenoid needed less than a dozen milliamps to run, she ended up choosing a budget all-in-one IC for the sake of simplicity and cost. She ended up choosing the [Texas Instruments DRV8871](https://www.ti.com/product/DRV8871) and picked up a [development board from Adafruit](https://www.adafruit.com/product/3190) to test it. She hooked up the two control pins to the Raspberry Pi and the motor driver voltage to the output of the boost converter. After writing some code to generate the opposing PWM signals for the H-bridge, the bell started ringing!

Finally, she needed to get audio input and output from the handset working. Hazel hadn't really worked with many pieces of audio electronics before this, so this is where the most was learned.

First was the question of how to get audio out of the Raspberry Pi. After some googling, she stumbled upon a simple solution: I2S DAC Amps. Turns out, the The Raspberry Pi has I2S PCM channels for both audio input and output. In software, PCM is one of the most common methods for storing and transmitting audio. With PCM simply being the equivalent of just sending raw sample values at the target sample rate, it would make sense that hardware devices would use it as a standard interface as well. She decided to order a [mono I2S DAC Amp development board](https://www.adafruit.com/product/3006) that's based on the [Analog Devices MAX98357A](https://www.analog.com/en/products/MAX98357A.html) from Adafruit to test out this theory. After the board arrived, she did some searching to figure out setup on the Raspberry Pi and stumbled across a [script from Adafruit](https://github.com/adafruit/Raspberry-Pi-Installer-Scripts/blob/main/i2samp.py) to perform all the setup automatically. After she ran the script and rebooted the Raspberry Pi, it didn't work. Using the ``aplay`` command to play an audio file resulted in an error: [INSERT THE ERRORS THAT I ENCOUNTERED AND THE RESOLUTION]. Finally, after running ``aplay`` again, she heard the audio coming out of the speaker, with better quality than expected! Finally, a demo of what was in store could be recorded:

[INSERT AUDIO DEMO VIDEO https://canary.discord.com/channels/772576325897945119/1052236377338683514/1265455539719114772]

Next, it was time to consider getting audio back in to the Raspberry Pi. Following in the steps of the I2S DAC Amp, she tried to find a I2S ADC to use. Sadly, this didn't return any helpful results and she couldn't find any boards or ICs capable of audio input that also had Linux ALSA drivers available. She had to think of a more creative solution to the problem, and that solution came in the form of a [random Amazon find](https://a.co/d/0bx4eb40). Since the phone already had very limited audio fidelity, she decided that taking a cheap USB sound card and cutting it up in to being a dedicated microphone input would be reasonable. After receiving the USB sound cards, she pulled the case off of one, cut off the audio jacks, and soldered alligator clips in their place so she could easily connect it to the microphone. She connected it to her desktop computer first for testing, but after recording herself speaking in to the handset in Audacity, the waveform ended up being blank. She tried reversing the contacts, maybe the microphone had a polarity? Nothing. She continued searching, trying to figure out why the microphone had seemingly zero output. Most results she could find mentioned electret microphones, but they also mentioned that electret microphones produce their own voltage with an internal driver that should result in even a faint signal through that audio input. After some more searching on the types of microphones used in older rotary phones, she found some mentions of "carbon film microphones". After searching for driver circuits for these microphones, she found mentions of needing to provide a bias voltage in order to get a usable signal. Turns out, carbon film microphones work completely differently from most modern microphones. Instead of measuring changing capacitance between a fixed plate and the diaphragm or directly generating voltage through the piezoelectric effect, carbon film microphones work on a much simpler principle. They measure the changing resistance of carbon particles sandwiched between two metal plates. This means that she needed a method to measure the resistance and turn it into a voltage that could be sent to the ADC in the USB sound card. She realized that a resistor divider would work perfectly for an application like this. A resistor divider uses two resistors in order to get an intermediary voltage between two voltage references. In this case, she had ground and 5 volts, and she needed an intermediary voltage between around zero and 1 volt. Since the carbon film microphone was already a resistor, simply adding a resistor in series with the microphone would provide the necessary bias voltage and create the resistor divider to allow the USB sound card to measure the microphone. While she could have empirically calculated this resistor value, she had a big bucket of random resistors on hand and so she decided to simply try a bunch and find one that sounded best. She picked a resistor out of the pile, a 5 kiloohm resistor. After building the circuit on the breadboard and hooking it up to the USB sound card, she hit record and spoke in to the handset. To her relief, she saw sound appearing. After playing it back, it was quiet, but very understandable. After trying a few more resistors, she found one that a 1 kiloohm resistor was basically perfect.

# Revision 1 Hardware

Now that all the individual components were together and working, she could finally lock down the electronics in to a more rugged and professional form factor: a Raspberry Pi HAT that would make it east to convert any ordinary Raspberry Pi in to a phone bell. Luckily, because everything was assembled as development boards and a breadboard, it was easy to translate over to a PCB. After creating the schematics for the dial and hook pull-downs, as well as the microphone bias resistor, as well as copying the schematics for the I2S DAC Amp and the H-bridge, she just needed to copy the boost converter circuit, but she ran into a snag: the schematic for the boost converter module she chose wasn't publicly available. This was a bit of a problem, she didn't have any experience building boost converters and had little knowledge of how they worked. Boost converters are a part of a family of DC-to-DC converters that use an inductor to perform the conversion. The cycle it follows is as follows: First the boost converter connects the inductor to the input voltage, causing the magnetic field in the inductor to start building. Once there is some energy in the inductor, the inductor is switched to being connected to the load with a series diode, this switch causes the magnetic field in the inductor to collapse which causes the voltage to shoot up. This cycle repeats as a high speed, and with some assistance from a capacitor on the output to help smooth out the voltage, it generates an output voltage inversely proportional to the switching frequency. After a bit of research, she found the IC used in the circuit, but there was still a question of the surrounding components and how they were connected. With a bit more research, she found some part numbers from some surround components, and a [document from Texas Instruments](https://www.ti.com/lit/an/slva372d/slva372d.pdf) on some of the math around calculating the needed components for the boost converter. After hours of double and triple checking calculations in the schematic to make sure it was correct, she felt confident that she had made a working boost converter circuit. At this point, it was time to add the USB Power Delivery circuitry. She had a USB Power Delivery negotiation board from a previous project that she was able to test here, and it worked successfully! After copying in that schematic, and adding some correctly rated Transient Voltage Suppression Diodes in places where ESD was a concern, she finally had a full schematic!

[PICTURE OF SCHEMATIC]

Next was the task of laying out and routing a PCB. Luckily, there weren't very many components and the board was relatively big. She first created the outlines for the hat perimeter, as well as a cutout for a cooling fan. She was worried that passive cooling wouldn't be enough in an enclosed space like the phone bell, and didn't want to deal with the mechanical quirks that would come from trying to use the metal chassis as a heat sink, so she added forced air cooling just to be safe. She first laid out the USB Power Delivery circuit on it's daughter board. She designed a drop-in replacement for the phone jack that would allow her to mount the USB Power Delivery board in it's place where she could then use a pair of JSTs on both the USB Power Delivery board and the main PCB to get power to the Raspberry Pi and the rest of the circuitry. She found an adequate components on LCSC and finished the board relatively quickly, joining the layout to the main board with mouse bites so it would be cheaper to manufacture and break apart during assembly. Next, was the main PCB, after finding the components it needed, she first placed all the JST connectors for the connections to the rotary phone components and their resistors close by. Next was the I2S DAC Amp, the datasheet for this IC had some recommendations for laying out the audio section to ensure the best performance, so after finding the rest of the supporting components it needed, she added it first. She then laid out the H-bridge circuit, luckily there were very few components so this was easy to slot in next to audio circuitry with a bit of a gap to prevent major interference. Now it was the time for the boost converter. She had found multiple mentions of specific traces needing to be as short as possible and certain components that needed to be physically close. After trying to keep that all in mind and doing a lot of shuffles to find a a good layout, she had something that she considered good enough. Finally, after a few checks to make sure she wasn't missing anything, she sent an order off to JLCPCB for fabrication, and an order off to LCSC for components.

Once the boards and components arrived, she sat down and assembled a pair so she could test them. After plugging in the USB Power Delivery board, she saw that it was successfully sending 5 volts to the board. Next was the moment of truth, after verifying that all the pins connecting to the Raspberry Pi were the correct voltages, she started her demo software. She played a sound file and heard the audio, success! She used Audacity to record from the microphone and got a clean recording, success! She dialed a number, and the software started showing numbers coming in, success! A problem immediately appeared though, the phone never started ringing after the number was dialed. The solenoid did move a little bit but it wasn't nearly as much as with the off the shelf boost converter. She started inspecting the board and noticed a faint clicking noise. After holding the board up to her ear, she noticed that the the boost converter circuit was making the clicking sound. While she was doing this, she let it get a little too close and it touched her ear. She suddenly felt something burning and jumped back. After calming down, she started touching around the boost converter circuit again and noticed that the coil was extremely hot. She immediately unplugged the phone to let it cool down. Once it was cool enough, she turned it back on to debug. She noticed that the output voltage of the boost converter was only around 16 volts, and the clicking sound was not something that happened on the original circuit. After a bit of research, she found two piece of information that gave her a clue as to what was happening. First of all, it turns out that you're supposed to add a holdout zone below the inductor to prevent it from accidentally acting like a tiny speaker with the changing magnetic fields. Second was a [document from Texas Instruments](https://www.ti.com/lit/ta/ssztak6/ssztak6.pdf) on debugging boost converters, and while she tried to inspect a few of the suggestions, without professional measurement equipment she wasn't able to come to a conclusive result.

At this point there were two solutions, either she could design more test boards and perfect the boost converter, or she could solder an existing boost converter to some of the pins to make it work. She originally started down the path of trying to make demo boards she could do testing with, but was cut short due to Purdue Hackers wanting to showcase the phone bells at their annual show, [BURST ✷](http://burst.purduehackers.com/). After realizing that she had 3 projects to prepare for BURST ✷, she decided to temporarily solder in an off-the-shelf boost converter and come back to making a revision two later. Once the boost converters arrived, she had to figure out how to connect them. She ended up piggybacking off the the trim potentiometer connections and the fan output to get all the power it needed. Finally, the phone bell was able to take inputs, handle audio, and ring the bell as a (mostly) fully integrated system.

# Revision One Software

The original software for the phone bells ended up being delayed due to the other BURST ✷ projects until there was only a day left before BURST ✷ started. With no software even close to working, she started a sprint. The first task on the agenda was getting the server code working. The phones needed some way to centrally manage the state of calls. She had previously written a server for other Purdue Hackers' services like the doorbell and events site in Rust with Axum. Instead of reinventing the wheel, she decided to add another endpoint that the phone bells could use. First was websocket upgrading. # Revision Three Software

As the phone bells sat in storage, Hack Night members became more and more annoyed at constantly having to run downstairs to open the door. Eventually, I cracked and decided to take another stab at finishing the project.

There were multiple problems to deal with, but the most pressing was WebRTC. My friend Ray told me about Iroh [LINK], a barebones peer-to-peer data exchange system. Iroh turned out to be perfect for our use case; we had just two devices sending audio to each other, so no need for complicated switching and mixing systems. With Claude Code’s help I moved the phone bells from WebRTC to Iroh, setting up a relay server for exchanging peer info aiding the Iroh Foundation’s discovery server in connecting the phones.  Hearing the first bits of audio come over the line was amazing, but there was still a long road ahead.

Audio is complicated. Audio over the network, even more so. I rediscovered this fact after Hazel had and it certainly got me into the weeds. I had various issues including audio becoming more delayed over time, dropped packets, and weird distortions. I traced all of the aforementioned issues to the way I was handling the microphone. The microphone was special due to how it was wired into the Pi, so there was specific processing code to get a precise audio signal from the handset. This code didn’t account for proper channel handling procedures (causing the distortions and occasional dropped packets) and was often starved by the other handset constantly transmitting packets (causing an accumulating delay).

Phones don’t just transmit voice, though. You usually hear a dial tone when you first pick up, a ringback tone waiting for the other person to pick up, and an off hook tone if you leave the phone off the hook for too long. We obviously didn’t have a professional phone routing system for this project, so all of the audio needed to be injected by us. This wasn’t too hard to do – injecting the tones works the same way as pushing voice through the handset. However, there was one thing I didn’t realize: the phone bells were configured for 16 bit wav files *only*. When I went to add another sound, I got a nasty surprise, or more accurately, nothing. I would play the sound on loop but no audio would come from the speaker. After wasting about thirty minutes on this problem, I put Claude Code on the case. After surprisingly few dead ends it figured out the resolution difference and successfully played the tone.

After getting the main audio system working, we could’ve deployed them and called it a day. Dial 0 on the outside phone, the inside phone rings, and once it’s picked up a two way voice channel activates. There was one thing missing, however: what about actually opening the door? This was the actual value add of the phone bells, the ability to let callers in without leaving Hack Night. Thankfully, most of the work had been done for me thanks to the door opener. All I needed to do was to tell the door opener to open when the inside caller dialed 0, and it worked!
